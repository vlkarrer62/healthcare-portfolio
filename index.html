Why provider onboarding is a product problem

When qualified behavioral health providers drop out before their first insurance appointment, patients lose access to care — not because demand is missing, but because the onboarding system failed them.

Patient access

Most people who need mental healthcare cannot pay out of pocket. Every provider who abandons credentialing is one fewer therapist, psychiatrist, or PMHNP available to accept insurance.

Provider supply

Onboarding asks for licenses, NPI, CAQH, malpractice documentation, payer enrollment, and practice preferences — often through repetitive forms with unclear status and silent processing states.

Conversion as design

The gap between interest and first appointment is not a marketing problem alone. It is a funnel problem: drop-off signals, trust breakdowns, and ops bottlenecks that need systems thinking — not another long form.

This portfolio does not claim shipped product outcomes. It shows how I would diagnose that funnel, define AI-assisted intake patterns, and prototype solutions worth testing with providers and ops teams.

Where I've seen workflows break down

Problem areas I have observed in clinical practice — and am exploring through workflow maps, product concepts, and prototypes.

Intake

Where trust is often won or lost — and where critical details go missing before care even starts. Unclear scope, repeated questions, and invisible next steps create friction for patients and teams.

Onboarding

Where providers and patients lose momentum because requirements, timelines, and blockers stay vague — especially across credentialing, enrollment, and care pathway setup.

Measurement

Where progress stays invisible between visits — so setbacks go unnoticed, improvement feels uncertain, and escalation arrives too late.

Handoffs

Where work moves between people, systems, and teams without clear ownership, required fields, or safe transitions — especially in high-risk clinical coordination.

Flagship Case Study · Concept Prototype · Provider Onboarding

AI-Assisted Provider Onboarding for Behavioral Health Clinicians

A concept prototype exploring how high-friction credentialing intake could become a guided, AI-assisted onboarding flow — with provider trust, data accuracy, and human review at the center.

Not tied to any single employer. Grounded in behavioral health credentialing patterns I have observed — CAQH profiles, state license verification, malpractice documentation, payer panel enrollment, and the gap between “application submitted” and “first insurance appointment booked.”

Problem

Behavioral health providers want to join an insurance-enabled care platform, but onboarding is high-friction. They may need to submit license data, NPI, CAQH information, malpractice insurance, payer enrollment details, specialties, availability, practice preferences, and identity documentation. The process can feel long, repetitive, unclear, and easy to abandon.

Users

  • Therapists, psychiatrists, PMHNPs, psychologists, and other behavioral health providers
  • Credentialing and onboarding operations teams
  • Product and support teams responsible for provider activation

Business / user tension

The platform needs complete, accurate, credentialing-ready data. The provider wants speed, clarity, and confidence. Patients ultimately need more available providers who can accept insurance. Design must serve all three without treating the provider like a data-entry endpoint.

Trust & safety language

Copy shown at high-anxiety moments — extraction, submission, and review.

At extraction

“I extracted these fields from your malpractice certificate. Review and confirm each one — nothing is submitted until you approve.”

At low confidence

“I'm not confident about the policy end date. Please verify or edit before continuing.”

At human review

“Your application is with our credentialing team. Typical review: 5–7 business days. You'll be notified if anything else is needed.”

What remains human-owned

Automation supports collection — these decisions stay with people and defined protocol.

  • Credentialing approval Final sign-off that a provider meets platform and payer requirements
  • License & NPI verification Primary-source validation when extraction confidence is low or data conflicts
  • Malpractice review Policy dates, coverage limits, and expiration exceptions
  • Payer enrollment routing Which panels to open and when a provider is released to accept patients
  • Exception handling Gaps, denials, and edge cases that don't fit standard intake logic

Design goals

  • Reduce repeated data entry across license, NPI, CAQH, and document uploads
  • Make onboarding status visible at every step — complete, missing, blocked, under review
  • Let AI assist with collection, extraction, and missing-item guidance
  • Require provider confirmation before any submission
  • Use human review for credentialing-critical decisions
  • Increase provider confidence and reduce abandonment
  • Avoid making AI feel like an opaque gatekeeper

Key design decisions

  • Confirm before commit. Extracted fields stay in draft until the provider explicitly confirms — nothing enters the credentialing record on AI output alone.
  • One status system, two views. Providers see blockers and next steps; ops sees the same underlying states in a review queue with assigned ownership.
  • CAQH and uploads work together. Pre-fill where data already exists; ask only for gaps — reducing duplicate entry without hiding what was imported.
  • Separate credentialing from activation. “Submitted to credentialing” and “ready for first appointment” are different milestones — conflating them erodes trust when payer enrollment lags.

Key interaction patterns

  1. Conversational intake

    A guided intake that feels like a structured conversation, not a long form — one clear question at a time with context for why it is needed.

  2. Progressive disclosure

    Only ask for the next necessary piece of information instead of overwhelming the provider with every credentialing field upfront.

  3. Review before submit

    Every AI-extracted field is shown back to the provider for confirmation before it enters the credentialing record.

  4. Status visibility

    The provider can always see what is complete, missing, blocked, or under review — no silent processing states.

  5. Human review checkpoint

    Credentialing-critical decisions are routed to operations staff, not silently decided by automation.

  6. First appointment readiness

    The provider sees exactly what must happen before they can accept their first patient — including payer enrollment and calendar setup.

What I would validate first

Before treating any concept as ready to build, I would pressure-test assumptions with providers, ops, and funnel data — especially trust, completion, and handoff to human review.

  • Provider interviews: Where do they hesitate, abandon, or distrust automation during credentialing?
  • Ops shadowing: Which fields most often require rework after submission?
  • Funnel baseline: Stage-by-stage drop-off from interest → start → document upload → ops review → first appointment
  • Trust calibration: Do providers understand what AI drafts vs. what humans verify?
  • Comparative test: Conversational intake vs. multi-page form on completion rate and time-to-submit

Honest limitations of this case study

  • Concept work — not tied to live product metrics or a paid design role
  • Funnel and metrics tables are illustrative models for testing, not reported outcomes
  • Prototype demonstrates interaction patterns, not production-ready engineering or compliance review
  • Would need payer-specific enrollment rules, state licensing edge cases, and legal review before launch

Research questions I would take into discovery

  • “At what step did you almost stop — and what would have kept you going?”
  • “What would you need to see before trusting AI-extracted credentialing data?”
  • “When ops is reviewing your file, what status updates actually reduce anxiety vs. create more?”
  • “What is the difference between ‘submitted’ and ‘ready for my first insurance patient’ in your mind?”

Concept Program · Behavioral Otology · Sole Designer & Builder

Attune — CBT self-management for tinnitus, hyperacusis, and misophonia

Attune is a CBT-based self-management program for tinnitus, hyperacusis, and misophonia — designed to close a real care gap while responsibly routing people to the right level of human support.

My role: sole designer and builder — problem definition, program architecture, interaction design, measurement design, and coded prototype. This is concept work informed by neurotology clinic experience, not a reported clinical trial or shipped commercial product.

Tiered support model — not fully self-serve, not clinic-only

Step 1Self-guided · 8-week iCBT
Step 2Group cohort
Step 3One-on-one support
AlwaysSafety routing out

The problem

Evidence shows CBT is the most effective treatment for bothersome tinnitus and disabling hyperacusis — yet in the Cleveland/Ohio area, there was nowhere to refer patients into structured CBT for sound-related distress. In neurotology clinic, otologists, NPs, and PAs evaluated these patients and often had to say, in effect, there's nothing we can do.

Clinic scene (anonymized): A patient leaves an appointment with validated severity on paper — tinnitus affecting sleep, concentration, and mood — and asks what comes next. The clinician knows CBT works. There is no local program to offer, no group to join, no digital pathway with accountability. The visit ends with a handout and a disheartened provider. That gap — not lack of evidence, but lack of access — is what Attune exists to solve.

Design reframe: This is a care-gap and access problem. The user is two people: the suffering patient and the provider with nowhere to send them.

Who it's for

  • Primary — patients with tinnitus, hyperacusis, or misophonia, often with co-occurring insomnia, anxiety, or low mood. They need structure, hope, and proof that change is possible — not another passive handout.
  • Secondary — referring clinicians who need a credible destination after diagnosis — something evidence-based they can recommend without overpromising a cure.
  • Tertiary — partners/spouses whose well-meaning accommodation can accidentally reinforce avoidance and hypervigilance around sound.

Emotional context

Distress, hopelessness, and hypervigilance shaped the tone of the program: calm and structured, not clinical-cold or wellness-cheery. Progress had to feel legible without shaming setbacks.

The central design tension

The formats invert. Group therapy retains and engages best but scales worst. One-on-one support is next. Apps and online programs scale best — but have the weakest engagement and highest drop-off.

Every Attune decision negotiates one question: how do you get near-group engagement at digital scale? The rest of this case study is my answer — a tiered model, not a binary between “app” and “clinic.”

Process & the pivot

  1. Started with a clinic. Initial intent: run CBT for sound-related distress locally. Observation: demand exceeded what one clinic could serve.
  2. Pivoted to a digital program. A structured 8-week platform could reach more people — but early framing skewed too self-serve.
  3. Corrected again. Some patients genuinely need human contact. “Fully self-serve” was the wrong absolute. Attune became a deliberate escalation ladder: self-guided → group cohort → one-on-one — with safety routing that exits the product when risk requires it.

These shifts are not indecision. Each came from recognizing that the right model is tiered support, not picking one format and forcing everyone into it.

Key design decisions

  • Evidence-based vs. engaging. Tradeoff: Andersson/Beukes-style iCBT is clinically faithful but can feel dense. Resolution: Beckian session structure, patient-authored weekly goals, and a completion tracker that makes progress visible — without diluting the CBT model into generic wellness content.
  • Program scope grew with observed need. Tradeoff: a narrow tinnitus-only program vs. serving related conditions. Resolution: expanded to hyperacusis and misophonia pathways, plus a partner/spouse module after seeing well-meaning partners reinforce the anxiety loop by over-protecting and accommodating avoidance.
  • Structure of the 8 weeks. Tradeoff: open exploration vs. accountable cadence. Resolution: weekly modules with patient-set goals, biweekly validated measures, and explicit review points — enough flexibility for individual goals, enough structure to fight drop-off.
  • When to add humans. Tradeoff: digital scale vs. isolation in distress. Resolution: group cohort for engagement; one-on-one for people who are really struggling — triggered by plateau, severity, or preference, not only by crisis.

Measurement as support

The measuring is the intervention — validated instruments track symptom burden; weekly goal completion tracks what CBT actually changes.

Standard tools (TFI, HQ, misophonia screen, ISI, PHQ-9, GAD-7, C-SSRS) capture severity — but not always the behaviors CBT targets: fear, avoidance, safety behaviors, confidence, return to normal life. Attune pairs validated measures with a CBT-sensitive weekly goal-completion tracker so measurement doubles as motivation.

Validated measures

  • TFI, HQ, misophonia screen at intake and every 2 weeks
  • ISI, PHQ-9, GAD-7 for co-occurring sleep and mood burden
  • Primary outcome (concept): change in TFI over 8 weeks

Honest landscape: tinnitus has stronger established outcome tools; hyperacusis and misophonia measurement is more fragmented — which is why the CBT-sensitive tracker matters.

Weekly goal examples

  • Walked outside 10 minutes × 3
  • Used sound enrichment at bedtime
  • Reduced checking / reassurance behavior
  • Sat in a mildly noisy room without escaping
  • Called a friend · completed one valued activity despite tinnitus

Safety & escalation design

Because Attune serves people in genuine distress, safety was a core design requirement — not a clinical afterthought. PHQ-9 item 9 and a C-SSRS-style screen are routing triggers, not passive data.

Screen result Product response Human pathway
Negative / low risk Continue standard self-guided pathway Routine program cadence · biweekly measures
Passive ideation · elevated distress Self-guided flow pauses Same-day human support · crisis resources · further risk assessment
Active ideation · intent · plan · recent behavior · cannot stay safe Exit self-guided mode entirely Immediate crisis support · emergency care · clinician review

Design principle: The goal is not to keep every user inside the product. The goal is to route each person to the level of care that matches their risk. Attune supports CBT-based self-management — it is explicitly not a crisis service, and the safety pathway makes that boundary visible.

Reflection

Attune taught me that designing for vulnerable users means holding engagement and responsibility at the same time — building enough structure to keep people in the work, and enough humility to route them out when the product is no longer the right container.

I would rather escalate someone to a human or crisis pathway than trap them in self-guided mode because the funnel looks better. What I'd build next: a coded app prototype of the 8-week flow, then a small group-cohort pilot to test whether the tiered model improves retention without diluting clinical fidelity.

Portfolio through-line

Attune and my provider onboarding case study share one design question underneath: where does automation stop and a human step in? In onboarding, human review for credentialing-critical decisions. In Attune, human escalation for risk. That boundary — visible, intentional, trustworthy — is the point of view behind this portfolio.

Honest limitations

  • Concept program — not a reported clinical trial or commercial launch
  • Measurement and safety frameworks are design proposals pending validation with clinicians and IRB-reviewed protocols
  • Group cohort and one-on-one tiers are architectural intent, not yet piloted at scale

Case Study 03 · Clinical Operations · Workflow Case Study

Reducing ambiguity in a high-risk ENT coordination workflow

A workflow case study drawn from ENT/neurotology clinical operations experience — documenting where ambiguity created risk, and translating that observation into proposed templates, checklists, and clearer ownership. The product problem here is often not a screen alone, but structure around the handoff.

From ambiguity to safer handoff

ProblemAmbiguity
StructureRequired fields
ClarityOwnership
OutcomeSafer handoff

Problem

ENT surgical coordination involves many handoffs across scheduling, pre-op, nursing, surgery, and post-op teams. Ambiguity around laterality, surgical approach, implant details, or ownership at each stage created risk for wrong-site events, delayed care, and repeated patient contacts.

Users

  • Clinical coordinators managing complex surgical pathways
  • OR schedulers and pre-op nurses
  • Surgeons and advanced practice providers
  • Patients navigating multi-step surgical preparation

Workflow map

Referral
Clinical evaluation
Surgical planning
Pre-op clearance
Scheduling
Surgery
Post-op follow-up

Each transition is a handoff point where information can be lost, duplicated, or misinterpreted.

Risk points

  • Unclear laterality (left vs. right) in referral and scheduling
  • Surgical approach not communicated to pre-op team
  • No single owner at handoff transitions
  • Implant/device details scattered across systems
  • Patient contacted multiple times for the same information

Communication template & checklist

In clinical coordination, I saw handoffs fail when laterality, ownership, and next steps stayed implicit. This case study proposes a structured handoff template requiring:

  • Confirmed laterality with source verification
  • Surgical approach and planned procedure
  • Named owner for next step
  • Device/implant specifications if applicable
  • Outstanding items with due dates
  • Patient contact status

Before / After

Before
  • Free-text handoff notes with inconsistent fields
  • Laterality confirmed verbally, not documented
  • Multiple staff contacting patient for same data
  • Pre-op team discovered missing details day before surgery
  • No standard escalation when information was incomplete
After
  • Structured template with required fields at each handoff
  • Laterality verified and documented at two points
  • Single coordinator owns patient communication
  • Pre-op checklist complete 72 hours before surgery
  • Clear escalation path when fields are incomplete

Why this structure matters

  • Fewer pre-op day-of surprises requiring last-minute calls
  • Lower cognitive load for coordinators — checklist replaces memory
  • Clearer ownership reduces “I thought someone else was handling that”
  • Fewer repetitive intake questions for patients

What I would test next

  • Template completion rates and which fields are most often skipped
  • Time-to-complete handoff before vs. after structured template
  • Near-miss and wrong-site event tracking over 6 months
  • Staff satisfaction and perceived cognitive load

Case Study 04 · Coded Web Project

Ohio Native Plant Guide — A structured educational web experience

A coded web project built with HTML, CSS, JavaScript, and JSON — organizing native plants, filters, habitat content, and educational pages. Included to show how I work with structured content, code, and direct prototyping outside of clinical case studies.

Content architecture pipeline

DataStructured JSON
DiscoveryFilters
DepthDetail pages
ContextEcological relationships

Problem

Native plant information for Ohio is scattered across field guides, extension PDFs, and nursery databases. There's no single resource that connects plants to wildlife relationships, habitat requirements, and practical growing guidance in a browsable, filterable format.

Users

  • Home gardeners interested in native landscaping
  • Conservation-minded homeowners selecting plants for habitat
  • Educators looking for structured reference material

Content architecture

  • Plant directory with individual detail pages
  • Wildlife relationship mappings (pollinators, host plants)
  • Habitat and growing condition filters
  • Educational content on ecological benefits
  • JSON-driven data layer separate from presentation

Structured data

Each plant entry in JSON includes: common name, scientific name, plant type, sun/soil/moisture requirements, bloom period, height, wildlife value, native range, and related species — enabling consistent rendering and filter logic without hardcoding content into HTML.

Directory & filter design

  • Card-based plant directory with thumbnail and key attributes
  • Multi-select filters for sun, soil, moisture, and plant type
  • Instant client-side filtering without page reloads
  • Empty state messaging when no plants match filters
  • Detail pages generated from JSON data

Visual system

  • Restrained, nature-informed palette — greens and earth tones
  • Clear typographic hierarchy for scientific vs. common names
  • Consistent card layout with attribute tags
  • Responsive grid that works on mobile field reference

What I learned from building in code

  • Separating content (JSON) from presentation (HTML/CSS) makes updates scalable
  • Filter UX requires careful empty-state and edge-case design
  • Building directly in code reveals constraints that mockups hide
  • Structured data forces content consistency — gaps become visible immediately
  • Coded prototypes are credible portfolio evidence for design-engineering hybrid roles

How I approach product problems

My process

  1. 01
    Start with the real workflow, not the screen.

    Map how work actually happens — handoffs, workarounds, and informal systems included — before prototyping any interface.

  2. 02
    Identify where users get stuck, uncertain, or unsafe.

    These friction points define the problem. Ambiguity, missing ownership, and invisible progress are often the real issue.

  3. 03
    Use structure to reduce cognitive load.

    Templates, checklists, required fields, and clear escalation paths replace the need to remember — especially in high-stakes clinical contexts.

  4. 04
    Make progress visible.

    Measurement, status indicators, and trend views help patients and teams orient themselves — and know when something needs attention.

  5. 05
    Define automation boundaries carefully.

    AI and automation should support clinical judgment, not replace it. Trust boundaries must be explicit, especially in healthcare.

  6. 06
    Prototype quickly, then refine with evidence.

    Use AI tools, code, and rapid prototyping to test ideas early — then validate with real workflow observation and user feedback.

  7. 07
    Keep clinical trust at the center.

    Every product decision should reinforce — not erode — the relationship between patients, providers, and care teams.
